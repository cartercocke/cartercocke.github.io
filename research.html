Research

Overview

My research interests mainly lie in computational solid mechanics and materials science. Computational modeling in these fields bridges mechanics, applied math, and computer science to form advanced models that predict complex material behavior. I find this intersection of fields exciting as a vast set of tools is required. While working on my thesis research at the University of Utah, I have worked with and developed FFT-based crystal plasticity models. These models simulate reversible and irreversible deformation processes that occur within polycrystalline materials and are a growing area of interest given their computational efficiency. I have recently focused on incorporating continuum damage mechanics into an FFT-based crystal plasticity model to predict failure within polycrystalline materials. Simulations with this model allow for the entire elastic-plastic-failure response of materials to be modeled, giving insight into how microstructure influences material failure. As FFT-based models are very computationally efficient, simulations can be modeled at higher spatial and temporal resolutions when compared with traditional finite element-based models. While I have only been pursuing research in computational sciences for about two and half years, I have found it to be incredibly fulfilling to learn about and apply topics in so many different areas. Consequently, I plan to pursue a Ph.D. following the completion of my M.S., and I am looking forward to what is to come!
