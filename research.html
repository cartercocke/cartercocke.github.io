Research

Overview

My research interests mainly lie in computational materials science and computational modeling. The field of computational materials science bridges mechanics, applied math, and computer science to form advanced materials science models. I find this intersection of fields exciting as a vast set of tools is required. While working on my thesis research at the University of Utah, I have worked with and developed FFT-based crystal plasticity models. These models simulate reversible and irreversible deformation processes that occur within polycrystalline materials and are a growing area of interest given their computational efficiency. I have recently focused on incorporating failure mechanisms into an FFT-based model to predict material failure based on microstructural features. Simulations with this model can be applied to better understand how material substructures influence the macroscopic response of materials and how those properties scale between different length scales. While I have only been working with these computational tools for about two and half years, I have many ideas for future projects, and I am excited for what is to come.
